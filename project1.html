Pythagorean Expectation for Sports Predictions

This project uses Python to forecast winning percentages in sports such as NHL, IPL, MLB, NBA, and English Soccer using the Pythagorean expectation formula. Here you can write in-depth details about your methodology, data sources, code, results, and insights.

Introduction

Recently, I read Moneyball by Michael Lewis—a story that brilliantly captures how data revolutionized baseball. Many of the chapters mentioned Bill James, a statistician who revolutionized baseball analytics since 1977. The book explained that the Moneyball philosophy is the idea that traditional statistics often overlook deeper truths in performance, and that undervalued indicators can reveal winning patterns. This is where Bill James came in to play in his creation of the Pythagorean Expectation. The Pythagorean Expectation estimates a team’s expected winning percentage based not on wins or standings, but on their scoring efficiency. How many times they score compared to how many times they get scored on. Though originally created for baseball, this has been put into play in other sports, and I wanted to test it out for myself with real life scoring data. How would different sports styles affect the formulas effectiveness? This led to the development of Pythagorean-Expectation-Sports-Predictions, a Python-based analytics project exploring how well this formula predicts winning percentages across five major leagues: • Major League Baseball (MLB) • National Basketball Association (NBA) • National Hockey League (NHL) • Indian Premier League (IPL – T20 Cricket) • English Soccer (4 different leagues) Through this project, I aimed to test the boundaries of one of sports analytics' earliest tools—by applying it to modern datasets, tuning it for different scoring environments, and comparing predicted vs. actual outcomes across varied sports contexts.

Background

The Pythagorean Expectation formula estimates a team's winning percentage as: Win% = (Score for)^2 / ((Score for)^2 + (Score against)^2) Where: • "Score for" and "Score Against" are cumulative season totals. • This model assumes that the relative scoring margin is a more stable predictor of performance than raw win-loss records.

Methodology

Each Jupyter Notebook in this repository includes: • Data Retrieval: Historical results were collected from Excel data sets provided free on the internet. • Feature Engineering: Calculated total points/runs/goals for and against per team and season. • Model Implementation: Applied the Pythagorean Expectation and used for predicting results in MLB and IPL. • Analysis: Compared predicted win percentages with actual results using regression techniques. • Visualization: Created scatter plots to examine the model fit. Takeaways and Conclusions

In exploring the Pythagorean Expectation across five distinct sports, this project reinforces how a simple statistical model originally conceived for baseball can reveal deeper patterns of team performance across different scoring systems and formats. The model performed best highly for four out five sports with cricket being the outlier. This is most likely due to the fundamentals of the two teams only getting to bat once each, however this could be analyzed further by potentially only taking home or away stats into account. Compared to today’s machine learning algorithms, Pythagorean Expectation is a mostly simple way of predicting results, however it showed that it can perform strongly with much less compute. As sports continue to embrace data analytics strategies, tools like the Pythagorean Expectation offer both accessibility and analytical depth, making them an crucial part of any sports data scientist’s toolkit.
